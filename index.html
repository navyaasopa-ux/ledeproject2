What COVID-19 Misinformation Patterns Tell Us:
Countries, Sources, Narratives
By Navya Asopa

Scroll down to track how rumors, conspiracies, and false reporting manifested across different global hotspots during the pandemic.

About the Dataset:

In 2020, some researchers at Princeton University analyzed over 5,600 misinformation stories of COVID-19 from around 80 countries in 35 languages. This data, collected between January and December 2020, explains some patterns: What kind of misinformation spreads, where it spreads, and who amplifies it?

Affected Countries:

While the pandemic was a shared global crisis, the misinformation it triggered was heterogeneous. China, the United States, Turkey, and India reported the highest volume of misinformation.

Social Media as Carrier:

Misinformation spread rapidly through social media, creating what the World Health Organization termed an "infodemic”. The primary channels were Facebook, Twitter, and Whatsapp.

Types of Misinformation:

There was a tremendous amount of regional variation in the nature of misinformation, dominated by false reporting, and then conspiracy theories.

Individual as the Source:

Approximately 80% of stories were spread by individual actors, while 17% originated from media outlets or political figures.

The Narrative:

Misinformation travelled for a wide range of reasons- the biggest one being spread of fear, followed by political motivations, and false hopes.
